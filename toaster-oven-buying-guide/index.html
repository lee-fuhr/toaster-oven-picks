How to choose the right toaster oven

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A toaster oven is a versatile countertop appliance that can replace a full-size oven for many tasks. For 2026, look for models with convection for even cooking, a large enough capacity for your typical meals, and easy-to-clean interiors. The right choice balances size, features, and your budget without going overboard on gimmicks.

What size toaster oven do I need?

Size is the first decision because a toaster oven that’s too small will frustrate you, and one that’s too large might overwhelm your counter. Interior capacity is usually described in slices of toast or cubic feet, but the real test is whether it can fit a whole chicken or a 9-by-13 pan. Most families find that a model holding four to six slices works for daily toast, small pizzas, and reheating leftovers. External footprint matters just as much. Measure your available counter space, including clearance above and behind for ventilation. A bulky oven that blocks the backsplash or sits too close to the fridge will be a daily annoyance. Many models now offer a compact footprint while still fitting a small chicken or a 12-inch pizza, so check the specs carefully. Weight and placement also play a role. Heavier models with more insulation tend to hold heat better but are harder to slide out from under cabinets. If you plan to store it when not in use, look for a design with a lift-up door that doesn’t require much overhead room.

Should I choose convection or a standard model?

Convection uses a built-in fan to circulate hot air, which reduces hot spots and cooks food more evenly. It’s especially helpful for baking cookies, roasting vegetables, and reheating crispy foods like fries or pizza. Many owners report that convection cuts cooking time by 20 to 30 percent compared to a conventional toaster oven. Standard (non-convection) models are simpler, quieter, and often more affordable. They work fine for toast, bagels, and basic reheating, but you may need to rotate pans halfway through baking. If you primarily make toast and frozen snacks, a standard oven might be plenty. But if you plan to roast or bake regularly, convection is well worth the extra cost. One trade-off: convection fans can be noisy and may blow lightweight foods like parchment paper around. Some models let you turn the fan off, giving you the best of both. Also, convection ovens tend to preheat faster, which saves time and energy.

Are combination toaster oven–air fryers worth it?

A toaster oven that includes an air fryer function essentially adds a super-convection mode with a high-speed fan. This lets you achieve crisp, golden results on frozen fries, chicken wings, and even whole vegetables with very little oil. For many households, an air-fryer combo replaces a separate countertop air fryer, saving space. That said, the air fry performance isn’t usually as powerful as a dedicated air fryer because the basket is typically smaller and the airflow less focused. You’ll often need to shake the basket partway through cooking. But for the versatility of having a toaster oven that can also do a passable job on crispy foods, the trade-off is small. Look for models with a dedicated air fry setting and a crisper tray or basket. Some units let you switch between convection bake and air fry with one button. If you cook a lot of frozen foods or crave fried textures without deep frying, this feature is worth prioritizing.

What heating element type is best?

Most toaster ovens use either quartz or metal-sheathed heating elements. Quartz elements glow red quickly and provide intense infrared heat, which is great for toasting and broiling. They preheat rapidly, but they can be more delicate and may break if bumped. Metal-sheathed elements (often made of stainless steel or Incoloy) are more durable and distribute heat more evenly, but they take a little longer to heat up. Many high-end models combine both types: quartz elements on top for broiling and metal elements on the bottom for steady baking. This hybrid approach gives you quick toasting and reliable baking. For most buyers, the difference is subtle, either type can produce good results. What matters more is the number of elements and their placement (upper, lower, and sometimes rear for convection). Infrared heating, which uses quartz tubes, can penetrate food more deeply and brown it quickly. If you frequently broil steaks or melt cheese on open sandwiches, a model with strong top quartz elements may be best. For primarily baking, even distribute from metal elements is more forgiving.

Which control interface is easiest to use?

Controls range from simple knobs for temperature, function, and timer to full digital touchscreens with presets. Mechanical knobs are intuitive and reliable: turn to the setting you want and you’re done. They’re easy to clean and less likely to fail over time. Many people prefer them for quick tasks like toasting. Digital controls offer more precision. You can set exact temperatures in degree increments and program custom cook times. Some models have memory buttons for your favorite recipes. However, digital interfaces can be fiddly, especially with greasy fingers, and touch screens may require more attention to operate. Presets (like ‘bake’, ‘broil’, ‘toast’, ‘reheat’) can be helpful for common tasks, but make sure they actually correspond to good time and temperature settings. Many owners override presets anyway. The best approach is to try a few models in a store or read owner feedback about how easy it is to adjust temperature or set the timer mid-cook.

What should I look for in cleaning and maintenance?

A toaster oven that’s hard to clean will quickly become a chore. Look for models with a non-stick interior coating, it makes wiping away grease and food residue much easier. An accessible, removable crumb tray is essential; you want one that slides out from the front so you don’t have to move the oven to empty it. Some higher-end ovens have self-cleaning or easy-clean cycles that use high heat to burn off residue, but these are not yet common. The door should be easy to remove or prop open for thorough cleaning. Avoid designs where food can drip into the heating elements, which causes smoke and smells. Also check whether the interior walls have exposed heating elements that collect grease. Models with covered elements are easier to wipe clean. Regular cleaning extends the life of the oven and prevents unpleasant odors. A good rule: clean the crumb tray after every use and do a deep wipe-down weekly if you cook with oil or cheese.

Frequently asked questions

Can I bake a 12-inch pizza in a toaster oven?

It depends on the interior width. Many large models accommodate a 12-inch pizza, but smaller ones max out at 10 inches. Check the interior dimensions before buying.

Is it safe to leave a toaster oven unattended?

It’s best to stay nearby while it’s running, especially for longer cooking. Many modern models have auto-shutoff features, but you should still monitor it.

Do toaster ovens cook faster than regular ovens?

Yes, because they are smaller and heat up faster. Convection models can reduce cooking times further by 20 to 30 percent.

Can I use parchment paper in a toaster oven?

Yes, but make sure it doesn’t touch the heating elements and is not draped over the sides. Always check the manufacturer’s guidelines.

What’s the best way to clean a toaster oven?

Use a damp cloth and mild detergent on the interior and exterior. Avoid abrasive scrubbers. Many models have a removable crumb tray for easy cleanup.

How much counter space does a toaster oven need?

Most models are about 15 to 20 inches wide and 10 to 14 inches deep. Allow extra clearance around the back and sides for ventilation.

In shortThe best toaster oven for you is the one that fits your counter space, matches how you actually cook, and is easy to keep clean. Focus on convection for even results, a size that handles your most common dishes, and controls you can use without a manual. With those basics right, the extras become nice bonuses rather than must-haves.
